# Operating Procedures

> How work is performed in the domain of personal training for overweight clients.

---

## Procedure 1: Client Onboarding & Initial Consultation

**Trigger:** New client inquiry or referral received.

**Steps:**

1. **Initial contact** — Respond to inquiry. Brief phone/email exchange to understand goals and confirm mutual fit. Set consultation appointment.
2. **Pre-consultation paperwork** — Send the client:
   - Personal information form (contact, emergency contact)
   - Health history questionnaire
   - PAR-Q+ (Physical Activity Readiness Questionnaire)
   - Informed consent / liability waiver
   - Personal training agreement (terms, cancellation policy, fees)
3. **Review paperwork** — Before the consultation, review all submitted forms. Flag any medical conditions, medications, injuries, or PAR-Q+ "yes" answers that require follow-up.
4. **Determine if medical clearance is needed** — If the client indicates cardiovascular disease, diabetes, metabolic conditions, joint replacements, recent surgery, or is on relevant medications, require written physician clearance before beginning exercise.
5. **Conduct the consultation (45-60 min):**
   - Build rapport. Use person-first language ("person with obesity" not "obese person"). Do not assume their goals are purely weight loss.
   - Discuss goals: short-term (4-8 weeks) and long-term (6-12 months).
   - Discuss exercise history, likes/dislikes, schedule availability.
   - Ask about previous weight loss attempts and what did/didn't work.
   - Discuss nutritional habits at a high level (within scope of practice).
   - Address any concerns or fears about training.
   - Tour the facility if applicable. Show equipment they'll use. Check weight limits on equipment.
6. **Conduct baseline assessments** (see Procedure 2).
7. **Set expectations** — Discuss realistic timelines, session frequency (recommend 2-3x/week), and the importance of consistency over intensity.
8. **Schedule first training session(s).**

**Output/Result:** Completed client file. Baseline data recorded. First session(s) booked. Client feels informed, safe, and motivated.

**Common variations:**
- Online/remote clients: consultation via video call. Assessments are self-administered with trainer guidance.
- Referral from healthcare provider: may include medical notes or specific exercise restrictions.

## Procedure 2: Baseline Assessment

**Trigger:** New client onboarding (after paperwork, before first training session or during consultation).

**Steps:**

1. **Select appropriate assessments** — Not all standard assessments are suitable. Choose based on client comfort and physical capability:
   - **Always:** Resting heart rate, resting blood pressure (if equipment available), height, weight, waist circumference.
   - **Recommended:** Body composition (bioimpedance scale, skinfolds if client is comfortable, or tape measurements at neck, chest, waist, hips, arms, thighs).
   - **Functional movement:** Bodyweight squat assessment (depth, knee tracking, balance), overhead reach, single-leg balance, wall push-up capacity.
   - **Cardiovascular:** 6-minute walk test (preferred over step tests or treadmill tests for deconditioned/overweight clients). Record distance and RPE.
   - **Optional:** Grip strength, sit-and-reach flexibility (use chair-based modification if floor-based is difficult).
2. **Progress photos** — Offer but never pressure. Explain they are private and used only for tracking. Front, side, back views in consistent lighting/clothing.
3. **Record all data** — Enter into client tracking system/software. This is the baseline against which all progress is measured.
4. **Communicate results** — Frame positively. Focus on what the data means for programming, not on judgment. Example: "Your squat depth shows us where to start building leg strength" rather than "You can barely squat."

**Output/Result:** Complete baseline data set. Starting point established for progressive programming.

**Common variations:**
- Skip skinfold measurements if client is uncomfortable with body contact.
- Use seated assessments if standing for extended periods is difficult.
- Defer some assessments to the first few sessions once rapport is stronger.

## Procedure 3: Program Design

**Trigger:** Baseline assessment complete. Ready to start training.

**Steps:**

1. **Determine training frequency** — Typically 2-3 sessions/week with at least 1 rest day between sessions. Recommend additional low-intensity activity on non-training days (walking).
2. **Design the initial program (Weeks 1-4):**
   - **Goal:** Build movement competency, establish habit, build confidence. NOT maximum calorie burn.
   - **Structure per session (45-60 min):**
     - Warm-up (5-10 min): Walking, light cycling, dynamic stretching (standing-based).
     - Resistance training (20-30 min): 4-6 exercises, 2-3 sets of 10-15 reps. Compound movements preferred. Use machines and bodyweight initially.
     - Cardiovascular (10-15 min): LISS — treadmill walking at incline, stationary bike, elliptical. Target RPE 4-6/10.
     - Cool-down (5 min): Seated/standing stretches, breathing work.
   - **Exercise selection priorities:**
     - Favor standing and seated exercises over floor-based.
     - Group any floor exercises together to minimize getting up/down.
     - Choose non-weight-bearing options where joints are a concern.
     - Avoid high-impact movements (jumping, running, burpees, box jumps).
     - Include core work through anti-rotation and bracing, not crunches/sit-ups.
   - **Suitable starter exercises:** Goblet squat to box, wall push-up, standing cable row, lat pulldown, leg press, seated shoulder press, step-ups (low step), farmer's carry, pallof press, dead bug (if floor work is tolerable).
3. **Plan progressions** — Define how to advance each exercise over 4-week blocks:
   - Week 1-2: Learn form, use light/moderate load.
   - Week 3-4: Increase reps or add a set.
   - Week 5+: Increase load, reduce box height on squats, progress push-up angle, add complexity.
4. **Plan regressions** — For every exercise, have a simpler alternative ready if the client struggles.
5. **Include breathing cues** — Overweight clients often breathe shallowly. Cue diaphragmatic breathing during warm-up and between sets.
6. **Document the program** — Enter into tracking software or write out clearly. Share with client if they train independently on off-days.

**Output/Result:** Written program for the first 4-week training block. Client understands the plan. Progressions and regressions prepared.

**Common variations:**
- Clients with significant joint issues may need a primarily seated/machine-based program initially.
- Clients post-bariatric surgery follow surgeon-provided exercise timelines.
- Remote/online clients receive video demonstrations for each exercise.

## Procedure 4: Training Session Delivery

**Trigger:** Scheduled session begins.

**Steps:**

1. **Pre-session check-in (2-3 min):**
   - How are they feeling today? Any pain, illness, sleep issues?
   - Any changes since last session (medication, injury, stress)?
   - Adjust session plan if needed based on check-in.
2. **Warm-up (5-10 min):**
   - 3-5 min light cardio (walking, cycling).
   - Dynamic mobility (arm circles, hip circles, ankle rotations — all standing).
   - 1-2 activation exercises (glute bridges if floor-tolerant, band pull-aparts).
3. **Main workout:**
   - Follow the programmed exercises.
   - Demo each exercise first. Use clear, simple cues.
   - Monitor form continuously. Prioritize quality over quantity.
   - Monitor RPE. Ask "How does that feel on a scale of 1-10?" regularly.
   - Watch for signs of overexertion: excessive breathlessness (can't speak in short sentences), dizziness, facial pallor, chest discomfort. Stop immediately if these occur.
   - Provide encouragement. Celebrate effort, not just outcomes. "That set was really controlled" > "You burned so many calories."
   - Allow adequate rest between sets (60-120 seconds, longer if needed).
4. **Cardio portion:**
   - Maintain conversational pace. If the client can't talk, reduce intensity.
   - Track duration and RPE. Build duration before intensity.
5. **Cool-down (5 min):**
   - Light walking to bring heart rate down.
   - Static stretching: major muscle groups used. Offer chair-supported or standing options.
   - Diaphragmatic breathing: 5-10 slow breaths.
6. **Post-session check-out (2-3 min):**
   - How did they feel about the session?
   - Any pain (distinct from muscle fatigue)?
   - Remind them of off-day activity (walking goal, stretching).
   - Confirm next session.
7. **Log the session** — Record exercises, sets, reps, weights, RPE, and any notes (pain, modifications made, mood).

**Output/Result:** Completed training session. Data logged. Client feels accomplished and safe. Next session confirmed.

**Common variations:**
- Some sessions may be primarily educational (teaching movements, discussing habits) rather than high-effort.
- Outdoor sessions: walking-based with bodyweight exercise stations.
- Virtual sessions: trainer demos on camera, client follows. Extra verbal cueing needed.

## Procedure 5: Progress Review & Program Update

**Trigger:** Every 4-6 weeks, or when the client plateaus/expresses concern.

**Steps:**

1. **Repeat key assessments** — Same measurements as baseline: weight, waist circumference, body composition, functional tests, progress photos (if client consents).
2. **Compare to baseline and previous review** — Note improvements, plateaus, and regressions.
3. **Review training logs** — Look at load progression, consistency, RPE trends.
4. **Discuss with the client:**
   - Share progress using objective data. Use graphs/charts if software supports it.
   - Highlight non-scale victories (strength gains, endurance improvements, energy levels, clothing fit, mood).
   - Ask what they've enjoyed and what they'd like to change.
5. **Adjust the program:**
   - Progress exercises that are now too easy.
   - Modify or replace exercises that cause discomfort.
   - Adjust frequency, volume, or intensity as fitness improves.
   - Introduce new movements to maintain engagement.
6. **Revisit goals** — Are original goals still relevant? Do they need updating?
7. **Document updates** — Record new program in tracking system.

**Output/Result:** Updated program for the next training block. Client sees measurable progress. Goals recalibrated if needed.

**Common variations:**
- If progress has stalled, investigate non-training factors (sleep, stress, nutrition, medication changes) within scope of practice.
- If client has been inconsistent, focus the conversation on identifying barriers and problem-solving, not guilt.

## Procedure 6: Referral to Other Professionals

**Trigger:** Client presents with needs outside scope of practice.

**Steps:**

1. **Identify the need:**
   - Persistent or new pain beyond normal muscle soreness → Refer to physician or physiotherapist.
   - Disordered eating behaviors, signs of depression/anxiety, body dysmorphia → Refer to mental health professional.
   - Need for medical nutrition therapy (diabetes management, specific dietary protocols for conditions) → Refer to registered dietitian.
   - New symptoms during exercise (chest pain, dizziness, shortness of breath at rest) → Stop exercise, recommend immediate medical attention.
   - Interest in bariatric surgery → Refer to bariatric physician/surgeon.
2. **Communicate the referral to the client:**
   - Frame it as expanding their support team, not as a limitation.
   - "I want to make sure you have the best support — a dietitian can help with the nutrition side in ways I can't."
3. **Maintain the relationship** — Continue training unless medically advised otherwise. Coordinate with the referred professional if possible (with client consent).
4. **Document the referral** — Note what was observed, what was recommended, and the client's response.

**Output/Result:** Client connected with appropriate professional. Trainer remains within scope of practice. Referral documented.

**Common variations:**
- Some trainers maintain a referral network (list of trusted RDs, physios, physicians) for quick warm referrals.
- In a gym/studio setting, some of these professionals may be on-site or affiliated.
