# Product Guide

## Vision

Get great results through strengthening the relationship and support between personal trainers and their clients.

## Product Thesis

Personal training results improve when the client feels seen, supported, and consistently guided between sessions.

The product should help personal trainers maintain stronger coaching relationships at scale. It should not start as another generic workout builder. The strongest early angle is helping trainers understand who needs attention, why they need it, and what kind of support would be useful right now.

## Positioning

A coaching relationship platform for personal trainers, helping clients feel supported between sessions and helping trainers know exactly who needs them most.

## Initial Segment

The first segment to explore is personal trainers who work with overweight clients, especially clients who are new to exercise or returning after a long break.

This segment strengthens the product thesis because the trainer-client relationship has unusually high impact:

- Many clients arrive with fear, shame, gym anxiety, or previous failed attempts.
- Progress is often invisible if the experience only focuses on body weight.
- Safety, exercise modifications, and scope of practice matter more than in generic fitness coaching.
- Trainers need to notice emotional, physical, and behavioral signals early.
- The client needs to feel respected, not judged or pushed into unsafe intensity.

The product should support this segment without making the client feel labeled by it. Internally, the app can model constraints, risks, preferences, and support needs. Externally, the client experience should use respectful, person-first language and emphasize confidence, function, energy, consistency, and strength.

## Primary Users

### Personal Trainer

The trainer manages several clients and needs a clear way to keep track of support, progress, context, and follow-up.

Core needs:

- Know who needs attention today.
- Understand what each client is struggling with.
- Prepare better for sessions.
- Remember important client context.
- Send timely support without spending all day in messages.
- See progress beyond physical metrics.
- Stay within professional scope around nutrition, pain, medical conditions, and mental health.
- Adapt training to client limitations, preferences, and safety considerations.

### Client

The client needs encouragement, clarity, accountability, and confidence that their trainer understands their life and goals.

Core needs:

- Know what to focus on today.
- Feel supported between sessions.
- Share how training is going with low friction.
- See progress and momentum.
- Trust that the trainer notices setbacks, wins, and barriers.
- Feel safe, respected, and not judged.
- Understand why a movement, modification, or habit is part of the plan.

## Product Principles

- Relationship first: every core feature should strengthen the trainer-client relationship.
- Support between sessions: the app should be most valuable when the trainer and client are not physically together.
- Low friction: check-ins, notes, and follow-ups should be fast enough to become habits.
- Attention management: the trainer should not have to manually inspect every client to know who needs help.
- Progress is broader than metrics: consistency, confidence, energy, stress, pain, and motivation matter.
- Human coaching, not automation theater: prompts and summaries should help the trainer act personally, not replace them.
- Safety and dignity are product requirements: avoid experiences that create embarrassment, shame, or unsafe escalation.
- Scope-aware coaching: help trainers recognize when to refer to a physician, registered dietitian, physiotherapist, or mental health professional.
- Modifications are valid paths: exercise alternatives should be framed as appropriate progressions, not failures.
- Trainer judgment leads: the app should surface signals, context, and possible concerns while leaving coaching decisions to educated trainers.
- Suggestions may be useful, but the trainer must keep agency over coaching decisions.

## Core Product Shape

The product should be centered around a coach-client relationship dashboard rather than a workout plan library.

### 1. Client Pulse

Clients submit quick daily or weekly check-ins covering signals such as:

- Mood
- Energy
- Sleep
- Stress
- Soreness
- Pain or discomfort
- Workout completion
- Goal confidence
- Free-text reflection
- Pain or discomfort location
- Exercise anxiety or confidence
- Barriers since last session

The trainer sees trends and risk signals, for example:

- A client missed two workouts.
- A client reports low energy several days in a row.
- A client confidence score has dropped.
- A client mentioned pain or injury risk.
- A client is struggling emotionally with progress.
- A client may need a referral or medical clearance before progressing.

These signals should be presented as prompts for trainer review, not as automatic conclusions about what the trainer must do. Suggested next steps are acceptable only when they remain optional and clearly trainer-controlled.

### 2. Relationship Timeline

Each client has a timeline of important coaching context:

- Check-ins
- Trainer notes
- Session notes
- Client wins
- Setbacks
- Goal changes
- Injuries or limitations
- Important life context
- Follow-ups and messages

The timeline helps the trainer remember context and makes the client feel seen.

### 3. Support Queue

The trainer dashboard should prioritize clients who need attention.

Examples:

- Follow up after a missed workout.
- Celebrate a consistency streak.
- Ask about knee discomfort.
- Review a drop in motivation.
- Check in after a stressful week.

This is the main product wedge: trainer attention management.

### 4. Session Prep

Before a session, the trainer should get a short brief:

- Recent check-ins
- Last workout or session notes
- Current goal
- Recent emotional state
- Pain or soreness signals
- Suggested conversation points
- Known limitations or preferences
- Safety flags and relevant medical-clearance notes
- Exercises to avoid or modify

The goal is to help the trainer arrive prepared and personal.

### 5. Client Home

The client experience should stay simple:

- Today focus
- Check-in prompt
- Latest trainer message
- Current goal
- Recent progress
- Encouragement or reflection prompt

### 6. Progress Beyond Metrics

Progress should include both objective and subjective signals:

- Strength
- Body measurements
- Weight, if relevant
- Attendance
- Workout completion
- Habit consistency
- Confidence
- Energy
- Stress
- Sleep
- Pain
- Motivation
- Functional milestones, such as walking longer, climbing stairs more easily, or getting up from a chair with more control
- Non-scale victories, such as improved mood, better recovery, clothing fit, and reduced gym anxiety

Weight should be one data point, not the headline.

## Safety, Sensitivity, and Scope

The product should help trainers deliver safe, respectful coaching, especially for clients who may feel vulnerable in fitness environments.

Important product implications:

- Intake should capture health history, medications, injuries, limitations, and comfort preferences.
- PAR-Q+ or equivalent readiness screening should be considered before training begins.
- Medical-clearance needs should be visible and hard to miss.
- Progress photos, body measurements, and weight tracking require explicit consent and sensitive handling.
- Pain, dizziness, chest discomfort, unusual breathlessness, or concerning symptoms should trigger conservative guidance and referral prompts.
- Nutrition features should stay within general habit guidance unless a registered dietitian is involved.
- The app should avoid shame-based language, punitive calorie framing, and public exposure of sensitive metrics.
- The app should avoid assuming exercise changes, referrals, or motivation strategies. It should make relevant context easy for the trainer to evaluate.
- The app should not make the trainer feel managed by software. It should support their judgment and reduce context-gathering work.

## MVP Scope

The first version should prove that relationship-driven support creates value for trainers and clients.

### In Scope

- Trainer account
- Client list
- Client profile
- Client check-ins
- Trainer support queue
- Relationship timeline
- Trainer private notes
- Basic trainer-to-client messages
- Session prep summary
- Simple client home screen
- Client intake and readiness profile
- Non-scale progress tracking
- Safety and referral flags

### Out of Scope Initially

- Complex workout builder
- Payments
- Nutrition planning
- Wearable integrations
- Exercise video library
- Marketplace features
- Group coaching
- Full calendar scheduling
- Advanced analytics
- AI-generated coaching without trainer review
- Medical diagnosis or treatment guidance
- Medical nutrition therapy
- Public progress-photo sharing

## Prototype Screens

1. Trainer dashboard: who needs support today.
2. Client profile: pulse, goals, timeline, notes.
3. Session prep view.
4. Client check-in flow.
5. Client home screen.
6. Client intake and readiness profile.
7. Progress view centered on non-scale victories.

## Pilot Success Metrics

- Clients complete check-ins at least 3 times per week.
- Trainers use the support queue at least 3 times per week.
- Trainers report improved session preparedness.
- Clients report feeling more supported between sessions.
- Trainers identify at least one client risk earlier than they would have without the app.
- At least 50 percent of support queue items are acted on during the pilot.
- Clients report feeling respected and not judged by the app experience.
- Trainers report that the app helps them discuss progress beyond body weight.

## Key Risks

- Trainers may not want another inbox or admin tool.
- Clients may not complete check-ins consistently.
- Support prompts may feel generic if they are not grounded in real context.
- The product could drift into a crowded workout builder category.
- Too much automation could weaken the human coaching feel.
- Privacy expectations must be handled carefully because check-ins may include sensitive health or life context.
- Health and nutrition features could accidentally cross professional scope boundaries.
- Sensitive measurements could harm trust if consent and privacy are not handled well.
- If the product feels like it is only for weight loss, it may undermine the broader relationship and health-support vision.
